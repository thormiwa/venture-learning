# Module 01: Dilution & Ownership

Welcome to the first module of Venture Learning.

Before learning how venture capital firms evaluate startups, write investment memos, or negotiate term sheets, it's important to understand one of the most fundamental concepts in startup finance: **ownership**.

Every startup eventually needs to answer questions like:

- Who owns the company?
- What happens when investors invest?
- Why do founders give away equity?
- Why do employees receive stock options?
- What is dilution?

Understanding these concepts will make every future topic in venture capital much easier.

# Equity

Equity represents **ownership** in a company.

If you own 100% of a company, you own all of its equity.

If you own 20%, you own 20% of the company.

Think of equity as ownership, not money.

---

# Shares

Shares are the units used to measure ownership.

For example:

Company shares: 10,000,000

Founder shares: 10,000,000

Founder ownership:

10,000,000 / 10,000,000 = 100%

When new shares are created, ownership percentages change.

---

# Dilution

Dilution happens when a company creates new shares.

Existing shareholders usually keep the same number of shares, but because there are now more total shares, their ownership percentage becomes smaller.

Dilution changes ownership percentages.

It does **not** necessarily reduce the value of someone's ownership.

---

# Example

Suppose you own:

100%

A venture capital firm invests in your startup.

After the investment:

Founder: 80%

Investor: 20%

You own a smaller percentage of the company, but the company now has additional capital to grow.

Owning 80% of a $50M company is often much better than owning 100% of a $1M company.

---

# Pre-money Valuation

The value of a company **before** new investment.

Example:

Pre-money valuation:

$4M

---

# Post-money Valuation

The value of the company **after** investment.

Example

Pre-money valuation

$4M

Investment

$1M

Post-money valuation

$5M

Investor Ownership

$1M / $5M = 20%

---

# Employee Stock Option Pool (ESOP)

An ESOP is a pool of ownership reserved for future employees.

Employees do not automatically own this equity.

Instead, stock options are granted from this pool over time.

Investors often ask founders to create an ESOP before investing so that future hiring needs are already accounted for.

---

# Stock Options

Employees usually do **not** receive shares immediately.

Instead, they receive **stock options**.

A stock option gives an employee the **right** to purchase company shares later at a predetermined (exercise) price.

Example

Today:

Share price = $2

You receive:

10,000 stock options

Exercise price = $2/share

Five years later

Share price = $50

You still have the right to purchase those shares for $2 each.

---

# Vesting

Stock options are earned gradually over time.

A common vesting schedule is:

- 4 years
- 1-year cliff

This encourages employees to stay with the company.

---

# 1-Year Cliff

Nothing vests during the first year.

If an employee leaves before completing one year, they receive nothing.

Once they reach one year, the first year's worth of options vest immediately.

After that, additional options continue vesting over time.

---

# Founders vs Employees vs Investors

Founders receive equity because they started the company.

Employees receive stock options because they help build the company.

Investors receive equity because they provide capital.

Everyone owns part of the company.

They simply earn ownership differently.

---

# Mental Models

## 🍕

Imagine a startup is a pizza.

The pizza = Equity

Slices = Shares

If more slices are added, your slice doesn't disappear.

Instead, your percentage of the pizza becomes smaller.

That is dilution.

---

## 💰 Equity as Startup Currency

Early-stage startups often cannot compete with large companies on salary.

Instead, they use ownership as another form of compensation.

Founders use equity to:

- Hire employees
- Reward early team members
- Raise capital from investors

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Dilution means losing shares.

✅ Dilution means your ownership percentage decreases.

---

❌ Owning less always means becoming poorer.

✅ A smaller percentage of a much more valuable company can be worth significantly more.

---

❌ Employees receive free shares immediately.

✅ Employees usually receive stock options that vest over time.

---

❌ Only founders get diluted.

✅ Investors are diluted in future funding rounds too, unless they invest more to maintain their ownership (pro rata rights).

# 📌 Key Takeaways

- Equity represents ownership.
- Shares measure ownership.
- Dilution changes ownership percentages, not necessarily value.
- ESOPs help startups hire and retain talented employees.
- Stock options give employees the right to buy shares later at a fixed price.
- Investors and founders can both be diluted over multiple funding rounds.
- A founder owning a smaller percentage of a much larger company is often better than owning 100% of a small company.
