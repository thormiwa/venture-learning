# How Do Investors Evaluate Execution Before a Company Has a Long Track Record?

One of the hardest parts of venture capital is evaluating founders before there is much evidence.

Imagine a founder pitches a VC. They have:

- No customers
- No revenue
- No product

There are no financial statements or years of operating history to analyze.

So how does an investor judge whether this founder can execute?

The answer is that **VCs look for proxies**—signals that indicate the founder is likely to execute well in the future.

---

## 1. Speed

One of the strongest indicators of execution is **speed**.

Investors ask:

> **How quickly can this team turn ideas into reality?**

### Example

A founder says:

> "I had this idea six months ago."

An investor asks:

> "What have you accomplished since then?"

### Founder A

> "We're still designing the product."

### Founder B

> "We've interviewed 200 customers and shipped three product versions."

Which founder appears to execute better?

Most investors would choose **Founder B**.

Execution is often measured by **how quickly a team learns and ships.**

---

## 2. Learning Speed

Execution isn't just about working hard.

It's about **learning quickly**.

Great founders don't become emotionally attached to their original idea.

Instead they say:

> "We believed X.
>
> Customers showed us Y.
>
> So we changed."

Poor execution often sounds like:

> "Customers just don't understand."

Investors want founders who respond to evidence rather than ego.

---

## 3. Recruiting Ability

Building a great company requires attracting exceptional people.

Investors ask questions like:

- Can this founder recruit world-class engineers?
- Can they convince talented people to leave comfortable jobs?
- Do other exceptional people want to work with them?

If outstanding people are willing to join the company early, that's a strong signal of execution.

---

## 4. Customer Obsession

Great founders understand their customers better than almost anyone else.

Chris Olsen described this as combining **domain expertise** with technology.

A founder who spent 15 years working in insurance probably understands insurance problems far better than someone who recently discovered the industry.

Investors look for founders who deeply understand the problem they're solving.

---

## 5. Storytelling

Execution is also communication.

Founders constantly need to convince people.

They must sell to:

- Customers
- Employees
- Investors
- Partners

Fundraising is sales.

Hiring is sales.

Leadership is communication.

The ability to clearly communicate a vision is an important signal of execution.

---

## 6. Small Wins

Execution leaves clues.

Even before building a large company, founders often demonstrate execution through smaller accomplishments.

Examples include:

- Building a side project
- Starting a community
- Growing an open-source project
- Organizing events
- Publishing research
- Launching products

Investors ask:

> **"Has this person successfully executed anything before?"**

The scale matters less than the pattern.

---

# Mental Model

Execution itself is difficult to observe directly.

Instead, investors look for **signals of execution**.

```text
Execution

↓

Speed

↓

Learning

↓

Recruiting

↓

Customer Understanding

↓

Communication

↓

Small Wins
```

Every one of these signals helps investors answer a single question:

> **Can this founder consistently turn ideas into successful outcomes?**

That's ultimately what venture capitalists are trying to predict.
