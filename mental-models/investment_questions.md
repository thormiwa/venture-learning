# VC Investment Questions

One of the most important skills in venture capital is **asking better questions**.

Great investors don't immediately decide whether a startup is "good" or "bad." Instead, they gather information, challenge assumptions, and understand what the company is optimizing for.

Use these questions whenever you're evaluating a startup, reading a pitch deck, or watching a founder interview.

---

## 1. Is the company intentionally sacrificing profits today to dominate the market later?

- Is the current lack of profitability part of the strategy?
- Do they have a credible plan to monetize in the future?
- Or are they simply unable to make money?

> **Mental Model:** Market Share Today → Value Capture Tomorrow

---

## 2. Can this company continue growing?

- How much of the market have they already captured?
- Is there still significant room for expansion?
- What does the next 10x of growth look like?

---

## 3. Which strategy fits my investment thesis?

Every investor has a thesis.

Ask yourself:

- Am I looking for companies that maximize market share?
- Or companies that capture value early?
- Does this company fit how I believe great businesses are built?

---

## 4. What is this company's moat?

- What makes this company difficult to copy?
- If it's open-source, what prevents someone else from commercializing it?
- If it's closed-source, what keeps customers from switching?

> **Question:** Why won't this company become a commodity?

---

## 5. How defensible is the business?

- What would happen if a larger competitor entered tomorrow?
- Could Google, Microsoft, Meta, or OpenAI replicate this?
- How durable is their competitive advantage?

---

## 6. Can they attract developers and build an ecosystem?

Especially important for developer tools and AI companies.

Ask:

- Why would developers build on this platform?
- Are there network effects?
- Does every new developer make the platform more valuable?

---

## 7. How large is the market?

A great company in a tiny market may never become a venture-scale business.

Questions to ask:

- How big is the Total Addressable Market (TAM)?
- Is the market expanding?
- Could this become a billion-dollar company?

---

## 8. Why do customers choose this company?

What is the core reason people adopt the product?

Examples:

- Lower cost
- Better experience
- Faster
- Easier to use
- Better technology
- Stronger community

If customers disappeared tomorrow, what would they miss?

---

## 9. Can this founding team execute?

Ideas are abundant.

Execution is scarce.

Questions:

- Can they recruit exceptional people?
- Can they ship products consistently?
- Can they adapt when things go wrong?
- Have they demonstrated resilience?

> **Mental Model:** Execution > Ideas

---

## 10. What has to be true for this company to return 100x?

This is one of the most powerful VC questions.

Instead of asking:

> "Will this company succeed?"

Ask:

> "What sequence of events would have to happen for this company to become worth 100x more?"

This forces you to think about:

- Market growth
- Product adoption
- Competitive advantage
- Founder execution
- Timing
- Technology shifts

---

# First Principles Questions

These are questions that help you think like an investor rather than simply judge a company.

- What is this company optimizing for?
- What assumptions is this company making about the future?
- Which assumptions are most likely to be wrong?
- What evidence would change my mind?
- What could completely break this business?
- Why might everyone else be underestimating this company?
- Why might everyone else be overestimating this company?

---

# The Goal

The goal of a VC is **not** to have all the answers.

The goal is to ask questions that reveal whether a company has the potential to become an extraordinary business.

Asking better questions leads to better thinking.

Better thinking leads to better investment decisions.
