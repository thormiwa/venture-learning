## Execution > Ideas

Ideas are abundant.

Execution is scarce.

A great team with an average idea often outperforms an average team with a great idea.

> "Innovation without execution is hallucination."

— Cohe Paul

## Open Source

Startups use open-source software because it lets them build faster, reduce costs, and focus on their unique competitive advantage instead of reinventing infrastructure.

Open source means: The creator makes the source code publicly available for anyone to see, use, modify, and often redistribute (subject to the license).

↓
"What new opportunities does that create?"
↓
"Who is building for that future?"

## One thing

Banks need repayment, VCs need upside, founders need growth, employees need motivation.

---

## Think in Assumptions, Not Predictions

Great investors don't try to predict the future with certainty.

They identify the assumptions a company is making about the future and ask:

- What has to be true for this company to become 100x bigger?
- Which assumptions are most likely to be wrong?
- What evidence would change my mind?

Investing is less about knowing the future and more about reasoning under uncertainty.

---

## A Startup Uses Equity as Currency

Startups often don't have much cash.

Instead, they use ownership to:

- Hire employees
- Reward founders
- Raise capital
- Acquire companies

Equity is ownership that can be exchanged for talent, capital, and strategic assets.

---

## Bigger Pie > Bigger Slice

Owning a smaller percentage of a much larger company is often better than owning 100% of a small company.

Don't optimize for ownership percentage.

Optimize for value creation.

---

## Dilution Isn't the Enemy

Dilution simply means your ownership percentage becomes smaller because new shares are created.

Good dilution should increase the overall value of the company.

---

## Ask "Why?" Before "How?"

Great advisors don't start with execution.

They start with purpose.

Before asking:

> "How do we IPO?"

Ask:

> "Why do we want to become a public company?"

---

## Build vs Buy

Building isn't always better than buying.

Sometimes acquiring a company is the fastest way to execute a strategy.

The question isn't:

> "Did we build it ourselves?"

The question is:

> "Did we create long-term value?"

---

## Open vs Closed Ecosystems

Open platforms often optimize for adoption.

Closed platforms often optimize for user experience and value capture.

Neither approach is universally better.

The right strategy depends on what you're optimizing for.

---

## Distribution vs Value Capture

Winning the most users doesn't always mean creating the most value.

Always ask:

"What metric is this company optimizing for?"

---

## Reason in Chains

Every investment thesis should follow a logical chain.

Observation

↓

Belief

↓

Reasoning

↓

Prediction

↓

Evidence that could prove it wrong

If you can't explain the chain, you probably don't understand the thesis.

---

## Great Investors Have Strong Opinions

The best investors aren't right all the time.

They're willing to form clear opinions, knowing they'll often be wrong.

One exceptional investment can outweigh many mistakes.

---

## Every Thesis Should Be Falsifiable

A useful investment thesis includes conditions that would prove it wrong.

Ask:

> "What evidence would change my mind?"

---

## Collect Observations Before Building a Thesis

Don't force yourself to have a thesis immediately.

Collect observations.

Look for patterns.

Develop convictions over time.

Strong theses are earned, not invented.

---

## Great Companies Are Execution Machines

Ideas start companies.

Execution builds enduring businesses. The ability to recruit, adapt, and consistently execute is often a stronger competitive advantage than the originality of the initial idea.

---

## The IPO Is Not the Finish Line

Going public is a milestone.

Remaining a successful public company is the harder challenge.

---

## Long-Term Investors Matter

The best shareholders are partners, not spectators.

Choose investors who support long-term value creation rather than short-term market expectations.

---

## Great Advisors Ask Better Questions

Advice is valuable.

Better questions are even more valuable. The best advisors help founders think more clearly before making decisions.

---

## Study Thinking, Not Predictions

Don't ask:

> "Was this investor right?"

Ask:

- What did they observe?
- What assumptions did they make?
- What were they optimizing for?
- What would have proven them wrong?

Understanding the reasoning is more valuable than memorizing the prediction.

---

## Venture Is a Power Law Business

Most investments fail.

A very small number of investments generate almost all of a fund's returns.

A VC's job is not to avoid failure. It is to find extraordinary winners.

Implication:
Judge a portfolio, not an individual investment.

---

## Banks Optimize for Certainty

Banks lend against predictable cash flows and assets. Venture capitalists invest in uncertainty.

The less predictable the opportunity, the less suitable it is for bank financing and the more relevant venture capital becomes.

---

## Technology Changes Economics

Technology isn't valuable because it's "cool."

It's valuable because it changes the economics of an industry. The best startups don't make existing businesses slightly better. They fundamentally change how value is created.

---

## Domain Expertise + Technology

Deep understanding of an industry combined with new technology creates exceptional startup opportunities.

The future isn't built only by software engineers. It is built by domain experts who leverage technology to solve meaningful problems.

---

## Startups Are Experiments

Every startup begins with a hypothesis.

The company's job is to test that hypothesis quickly and continuously. The market decides whether the hypothesis is correct.

---

## Great VCs Build Conviction Before Everyone Else

Instead of waiting for great startups to appear, they study industries, identify trends, meet founders early, and develop conviction before the rest of the market notices.
